Final Reflection

Creating both visualizations provided a valuable opportunity to explore how data transformations and framing can powerfully influence interpretation. The most straightforward part of the design process was the technical implementation—constructing clear, visually clean line charts with proper labeling, consistent color schemes, and shape encoding for clarity. However, the conceptual work—deciding what to show, what to omit, and how to structure the narrative—proved much more nuanced. Choosing between absolute counts and proportional rates, and grappling with the implications of each, was particularly challenging. The exercise revealed how drastically a shift in metric can reshape a viewer’s understanding, even when the underlying dataset remains constant.

What surprised me most was how subtle design decisions—like axis scaling, annotation tone, or even the title—can dramatically influence perceived neutrality or bias. Through this process, my understanding of “ethical analysis and visualization” has evolved. I now see it as the careful balance between truthful representation and rhetorical clarity. Ethical visualization involves being honest not only in the data shown but also in what’s left out and why. Acceptable persuasive choices are those rooted in transparency and context: you can emphasize a point, but you must also equip viewers with the tools to question and interpret it themselves. Misleading choices, by contrast, hide or obscure critical context—such as denominators in rates or dataset composition—in order to push a singular, often unjustified conclusion.

Ultimately, the line between persuasive and deceptive lies in intent and disclosure. Persuasion that encourages critical engagement is ethical; persuasion that inhibits it is not. This project reaffirmed the responsibility that comes with data storytelling: the visual choices we make aren’t neutral, and their implications can shape public perception in profound ways.
